# Search Strategies for Company Discovery

> How to find companies that generic aggregators miss. Every agent should read this before beginning their search — then go beyond it.

---

## The core problem

Obvious searches produce obvious results. Searching "[sector] companies [location]" returns the same list every recruiter and job board already has. These companies are worth knowing about, but they are not where discovery adds value. The value is in finding companies the user would never have encountered through normal job searching.

This requires looking in places where companies leave traces of their work without explicitly advertising themselves as employers.

The strategies below are approaches to discovery, not exhaustive source lists. The specific sources you search depend on the profile — the user's skills, technologies, domains, and location preferences determine where to look. Read the profile first, then apply these strategies to find sources relevant to that profile.

---

## Strategy 1: Curated lists and investor portfolios

The most straightforward source. Useful for building the baseline, but should not be the only strategy.

**The approach:** Venture capital firms publish portfolio pages listing every company they have backed, often with brief descriptions and sector tags. Industry ranking sites publish curated lists by sector. Startup databases aggregate funding and sector data.

**How to find relevant sources:** Search for VC firms that invest in the sectors the profile targets. Search for "[sector] startup rankings", "[sector] company lists", and "[location] tech ecosystem" to find curated lists relevant to the profile's geography and domain interests. Look for sector-specific databases and directories — every industry has them.

**How to use well:** Do not just grab names. Read the portfolio descriptions and filter for companies whose work genuinely connects to the profile. A VC portfolio page might list 80 companies, but only a handful will do work where the profile's specific skills are valued. The filtering is where the judgment lives.

---

## Strategy 2: Open source and code ecosystem signals

Companies that build in the open leave traces in the ecosystem. These traces are high-signal because they reveal what the company actually works on, not just what their marketing says.

**The approach:** Identify the key open source projects, libraries, and frameworks relevant to the profile's technologies. Look at who contributes to them — not just who owns the repository, but who is actively committing code. Contributors' profiles and commit emails reveal their employers. Companies that pay engineers to contribute to ecosystem tools are building serious infrastructure around those technologies.

**How to find relevant sources:** From the profile's technology stack, identify the major open source dependencies and ecosystem projects. Search for trending repositories in those languages and frameworks. Look for "awesome-[technology]" lists, foundation member directories, and ecosystem landscape maps relevant to the profile's domains.

**How to use well:** Look beyond repository ownership to contribution patterns. A company with multiple engineers contributing to a key library is building real infrastructure around that technology, even if their name never appears on a "top startups" list. Also look at GitHub organisation pages of companies you have already identified — their public repos reveal what they actually build.

---

## Strategy 3: Conference and event sponsors

Companies that sponsor or speak at technical conferences are actively investing in the technologies and domains they sponsor. Sponsor lists are public and often include company descriptions.

**The approach:** Identify conferences and meetups relevant to the profile's technologies and domains. Check sponsor lists, speaker company affiliations, and talk abstracts.

**How to find relevant sources:** Search for conferences in the profile's target technologies, domains, and geographies. Look for both large international conferences and smaller local meetups — smaller events often feature companies that larger events miss. Search for "[technology] conference", "[domain] summit", and "[location] tech meetups".

**How to use well:** Speaker company affiliations are especially valuable. An engineer giving a talk about building something in the profile's target domain reveals exactly what their company works on. The talk abstract often has more technical detail than the company's website.

---

## Strategy 4: Community discussions and hiring threads

"Who's hiring" threads surface companies that are actively looking, often with context that job boards strip away — what team, what stage, what the actual work involves.

**The approach:** Search community forums for hiring threads, job discussions, and company mentions. These threads provide context that structured job boards cannot — team size, technical stack, growth stage, and sometimes visa sponsorship details.

**How to find relevant sources:** Identify the online communities where practitioners in the profile's domains congregate. Every technology and sector has its forums, subreddits, mailing lists, and discussion boards. Search for "[technology] jobs", "[domain] hiring", and "who is hiring" within those communities. Use searchable archives where available and filter by the profile's target location and technologies.

**How to use well:** The unstructured context in these threads is gold. A community post describing team size, technical challenges, growth trajectory, and hiring needs contains more signal than any job board listing. Pay attention to the details people share when they are trying to attract engineers, not just fill a requisition.

---

## Strategy 5: Funding and investment signals

Recently funded companies are the most likely to be actively hiring. Funding announcements often describe what the company does in more detail than their website.

**The approach:** Search for recent funding rounds in the profile's target sectors and geographies. Focus on the round description — what the company does and what they plan to build — rather than the amount raised.

**How to find relevant sources:** Use startup databases and funding trackers filtered by sector and location. Search for "[sector] funding rounds [location]", "recently funded [domain] companies". Check VC firm blogs and social media for portfolio announcements. Look for government investment announcements in the profile's target sectors.

**How to use well:** Focus on the round description, not just the amount. A company that raised money "to build next-generation infrastructure for [domain]" is more relevant than one that raised ten times as much "to expand our consumer app." The what matters more than the how much.

---

## Strategy 6: Engineering blogs and technical content

Companies that publish engineering blogs are revealing their technical stack, their problems, and their culture. This is the highest-fidelity signal for assessing whether a company does work the profile aligns with.

**The approach:** Search for engineering blog posts about the technologies and problems relevant to the profile. Individual engineers writing about their work reveal their employer, their stack, and the nature of the technical challenges.

**How to find relevant sources:** Search for blog posts about the profile's key technologies, e.g. "[technology] in production", "building [system type]", "[domain] engineering". Check engineering blog aggregators and technical publication platforms. Look at individual engineer profiles on technical writing platforms — their employer information is usually visible.

**How to use well:** An engineering blog post describing a technical challenge in a domain the profile targets tells you: the company works in that domain, they use technologies the profile knows, they are building at a scale that produces interesting problems, and they value engineering culture enough to write about it. Each of those signals matters.

---

## Strategy 7: Sector-specific deep dives

For each sector in the profile, there are domain-specific sources that general tech lists do not cover.

**The approach:** Identify the regulatory bodies, industry associations, ecosystem participants, research institutions, and domain-specific directories for each of the profile's target sectors. These sources surface companies that exist entirely outside the mainstream tech ecosystem's awareness.

**How to find relevant sources:** This is where profile-driven creativity matters most. Every sector has its own ecosystem of directories, regulators, associations, and landscape maps. Search for "[sector] companies [location]", "[sector] association members", "[sector] ecosystem map", "[sector] landscape". Look for regulatory registers, industry body member lists, and domain-specific databases. A profile targeting financial services should search different sources than one targeting biotech or developer tooling.

**How to use well:** Domain-specific sources surface companies that have no presence in the general tech ecosystem. A company on a financial regulator's register, or a member of a biotech industry association, may be doing highly technical work that perfectly matches the profile but would never appear on a generic "top tech companies" list.

---

## Strategy 8: The "who else" expansion

Once you find one good company, ask: who are their competitors, their partners, their customers, and where do their alumni go?

**The approach:**
- Search "[company name] competitors" or "[company name] alternatives"
- Look at the company's partner and integration pages
- Check comparison and review sites for related companies
- Search for the company on employer review sites — comparison features show related companies
- Look at employee movement patterns — where do engineers at this company come from and go to?

This is the most effective strategy for finding non-obvious companies. One good discovery leads to several more through proximity. Apply it to every strong find.

---

## Inventing new strategies

The strategies above are established approaches. They are not the only approaches. The best discovery agents find companies through methods this document does not describe.

Think about what traces the profile's target companies leave in the world. Where do they advertise? Where do their engineers publish? What ecosystems do they participate in? What supply chains are they part of? What events do they attend? What standards bodies do they contribute to? What partnerships do they announce?

Every trace is a potential discovery source. The strategies above cover the common trace types. The uncommon ones — the ones specific to the profile's particular combination of skills, domains, and geographies — are where the most valuable discoveries live.

---

## Combining strategies

No single strategy covers the full landscape. The most productive approach combines:

1. **Curated lists** for the baseline — the known names that should be in any universe
2. **Code ecosystem signals** for companies building with the specific technologies the profile uses
3. **Funding signals** for companies that are actively growing and likely hiring
4. **Engineering blogs and conference talks** for companies doing the exact technical work the profile aligns with
5. **Community threads** for companies hiring right now with real context
6. **The "who else" expansion** for turning each good find into a cluster of related companies
7. **Sector-specific sources** for depth in the domains that matter most
8. **Novel strategies you invent** for the finds nobody else would make

The first three strategies build breadth. The rest build depth. Both matter.
