## 2. Methodology

I worked from the profile files first, then used the rubric as a reasoning scaffold, then web-researched 2026 state. My biases and assumptions were:

- **Visa accessibility is a Tier-1 dealbreaker that is often mis-weighted.** Most city rankings lean on "firm density" and treat visa as background. For a Turkish passport holder with August 2027 as a hard clock, visa mechanics are often the *dominant* factor — a visa path that works for Indians and Nigerians at BigCo may not work for a fresh Turkish BEng with no work history. I repeatedly pushed cities down the list where the visa pathway is technically available but practically narrow at entry level.
- **The 2027 clock forces a bifurcated plan.** The analysis must answer two questions: "where should Caner go in 2027 if he is still on UK soil?" and "where could Caner go *now* if a compelling offer materialised?" These have different answers. Short-term, London dominance is so complete it borders on trivial. Medium-term, the question becomes "where do you land when the Graduate visa ends?" and this is where most of the interesting analysis happens.
- **I took the profile's lifestyle preferences seriously but refused to treat them as hard filters.** The Amsterdam rejection in particular struck me as premature given Optiver/IMC/Flow Traders density. Height friction is real but a 24-year-old with a globally mobile career aspiration is going to need to accept *some* environmental compromise in exchange for career acceleration, and Amsterdam's compromise is milder than most.
- **I weighted trajectory heavily.** Berlin's current state is better than its 5-year vector. UK's current state is worse than its 5-year vector (nightlife revival, Agent of Change licensing reform, post-Brexit stabilisation). Dublin's housing trajectory is concerning. Sydney's climate trajectory is concerning. Zurich's quota trajectory is flat (unchanged for 2026). These vectors moved my rankings.
- **I refused to let "prestigious firm" substitute for "fits the profile."** Meta Dublin exists. Google Dublin exists. AWS Dublin exists. None of them are what Caner should optimise for — his portfolio is oriented toward systems, Rust, quant, AI infra, and local-first desktop, and FAANG product engineering roles grade B at best against his actual interests. I leaned heavily toward firms where his Rust/systems/quant portfolio is a *differentiator*, not where it's merely *acceptable*.
- **I assumed the career goal is a non-negotiable constraint.** The cover letter mentions AI-and-health as a long-term interest, but the near-term portfolio is overwhelmingly systems/Rust/quant/fintech. Cities were evaluated against the near-term direction, because that's where the next 3-5 years of income and skill compounding will happen.

Sources were a mix of official government immigration portals (canada.ca, nyidanmark.dk, ind.nl, make-it-in-germany.com, immi.homeaffairs.gov.au), 2026-dated immigration law firm updates (Fragomen, Grant Thornton, Jobbatical, Davidson Morris), and current news on political trajectories (BBC, Reuters, The Local). Full URL list at the end.

## 3. Continent-by-Continent Sweep

### Europe

**Framing.** Europe is Caner's default continent. Language and culture proximity to Turkey, existing UK residency, a functional Schengen-adjacent integration story, and the densest concentration of relevant firms outside the US. European visa mechanics for Turkish nationals are broadly workable — the EU Blue Card, Ireland's CSEP, Austria's RWR Card, and national schemes in Sweden, Denmark, and the Netherlands all admit skilled third-country nationals at graduate salary levels. Nothing in Europe is as mechanically hard as the US H-1B lottery or the Canadian Express Entry points system.

#### United Kingdom — London

London is the reigning incumbent and the only city where Caner is *already* competitive without relocation. Firm density is the unmatched global anchor for quant/HFT in Europe: Jane Street London, HRT London, Citadel, Optiver London, IMC London, XTX Markets, Jump, Millennium, Squarepoint, G-Research. Fintech: Stripe, Revolut, Thought Machine, Wise, Monzo, Starling. AI infra: DeepMind, Anthropic London, Isomorphic Labs, Wayve, Graphcore (Bristol but UK). Rust-in-production: TrueLayer, Evervault, Cloudflare London, Proton UK presence.

The London tech job market in early 2026 is recovering from the 2023-2024 layoffs — financial services hiring was up 9% YoY in Q3 2025 (FinTech Weekly) and the graduate quant pipeline is active. Jane Street paid entry-level engineers some of the highest salaries in the world. Stripe's London intern programme is the single highest-probability high-prestige landing Caner can target without a passport change.

The visa reality: the Graduate visa runs out August 2027. The Skilled Worker salary threshold rose to £41,700 general / higher going rates from 22 July 2025, and to be a "new entrant" discount the role needs to fit specific categories. A quant/HFT graduate programme in London pays well above threshold; a startup with no sponsor licence does not. Caner should explicitly avoid unsponsored small fintech startups in his 2025-2027 applications, because they kick the visa problem down the road without resolving it.

Aesthetically London is the reference point that defines Caner's preferences — Kings Cross, Nine Elms, Paddington, Battersea. Safety at the Croydon level is a live pain point, but this is fixable with a move *within* London, not by leaving London. It's worth pointing out that even the Croydon safety issue is solvable on a Jane Street or HRT graduate salary — he should bank the first year's compensation into a Central London flat the moment income permits.

Verdict: **stay unless a clearly superior offer materialises**. London is the baseline the rest of the list is compared against. Trajectory: mildly positive on the 1-3 year horizon (AI regulation stabilising, fintech hiring recovery), neutral-positive on 5-7 year (cost of living crushing, but firm density will not move), uncertain on 10-15 year (Brexit damage mostly done; climate exposure modest).

#### Ireland — Dublin

Dublin is the single most visa-accessible European destination for a fresh Turkish BEng with no work history. The Critical Skills Employment Permit at the *graduate* threshold is €36,848 (from 1 March 2026, per Ireland's 2026 roadmap) — that is achievable at Stripe Dublin, Susquehanna Dublin, Intercom, Fenergo, Workday, Qualtrics, and any of the big AI labs with Dublin offices (Google, Meta, Accenture Labs). Stamp 4 (full residency) after 2 years is one of the fastest PR paths in Europe. There is no language hurdle.

Dublin's tech cluster is Stripe-adjacent fintech (huge), US cloud regional HQs (Google, Meta, AWS EMEA), AI research satellites (Google DeepMind Dublin does not exist but Google Dublin has research teams), and a real but thinner quant cluster (Susquehanna Dublin, Citadel Dublin has a presence, Akuna Capital). The 2026 tech layoff wave hit Dublin hard — Meta laid off its Dublin engineering staff in rolling rounds through 2024-2025, Stripe did a 14% global cut but kept Dublin HQ. The firm density is about 60% of London's for Caner's sectors, but the visa advantage more than compensates for a graduate-stage move.

Lifestyle: Dublin central (Grand Canal Dock, Docklands) is modern, safe enough with urban awareness, and English-speaking. It is smaller than London and the regenerated districts are more modest in scale — no KGX-equivalent, more like "central London in a smaller city." The pub-centric social scene is very different from continental Europe and somewhat in line with Caner's "decent, not elite" nightlife bar. Café culture exists but is weaker than London, which matters for his working style. Housing is a genuine crisis — Dublin rents have inflated faster than Stockholm or Berlin, and a graduate salary stretches thin if you want Grand Canal Dock proximity.

Verdict: **strong backup plan if he does not clear the London sponsorship gate by 2027**. Worth direct-applying to in parallel through 2025-2027 as cover. Stripe Dublin in particular is an SS-tier landing spot. Trajectory: Ireland's fiscal surplus makes the state solvent, the corporate tax regime is holding after the 15% minimum tax, AI investment continues inflating the Dublin cluster. Housing crisis is the one degrading axis and it is sharp.

#### Netherlands — Amsterdam

**I am rejecting the prior "rejected" framing and re-evaluating from zero.** The case for Amsterdam as a top-tier destination for Caner is, honestly, stronger than I expected when I started.

The firm density is the argument. Optiver is headquartered in Amsterdam. IMC is headquartered in Amsterdam. Flow Traders is headquartered in Amsterdam. Da Vinci Derivatives, MakoGlobal, and numerous smaller prop shops cluster here. Booking.com, Adyen (arguably the strongest European fintech infrastructure company), Bitvavo. Amsterdam is the only European city outside London where the quant/HFT graduate pipeline is this dense, and because it is the *headquarters* rather than a satellite, the technical roles are the actual primary engineering work rather than the second-tier support work that often characterises London office roles of Amsterdam-HQ firms.

The visa is genuinely friendly. The Highly Skilled Migrant under-30 threshold is €4,357/mo (~€52,284/yr) but a reduced graduate threshold of €3,122/mo (~€37,464/yr) exists specifically for recent graduates, which Caner would qualify for. Processing is fast by European standards. The 30% ruling gives skilled migrants a 30% effective tax reduction on taxable salary for the first 5 years, which makes the post-tax figures competitive with London and Zurich despite the nominal 49.5% top rate.

The lifestyle concerns I need to take seriously:

- **Height**: the Dutch male average is ~184cm; Caner is 170cm. This matters in daily physical environment scaling (bar stool heights, tables, bicycles, shower heads). It is not imaginary, but it is not a binary disqualifier — plenty of 170cm men work in Amsterdam and live without incident. This is a real friction factor, worth ~5-10% overall fit reduction, not a veto.
- **Aesthetic**: Amsterdam's historic centre (Jordaan, Canal Ring) is exactly the low-rise historic type Caner explicitly rejects. But Amsterdam is not *only* its historic centre — Zuidas is the modern financial district and has exactly the mixed-scale regeneration Caner grades A+ elsewhere, and it is where Optiver/IMC/Flow Traders and the Zuidas offices of big law/finance sit. Living in Zuidas or Oost (a regenerating mixed-use area) is a genuine option and the aesthetic arguments soften considerably.
- **Body-scale tables, bikes, environment**: it is what it is. He can adapt.

Secular culture is strong (Netherlands is one of the most secular societies in Europe). Safety is high in Zuidas and central. Café culture with laptops is mixed — Dutch cafés are friendlier to laptop work than French ones but less so than London or Berlin. English is ~95% functional in Amsterdam daily life. Waymo is not there but the Dutch transport/tech adoption is high.

Verdict: **top 3 candidate for a 2027 move**. The profile's prior rejection was overweighting the lifestyle friction and underweighting the career-accelerator argument. Amsterdam is where Caner should *stop and seriously consider* if an Optiver or IMC graduate offer materialises. Trajectory: housing pressure is real but less extreme than Dublin; the Netherlands announced "tightening" of the Highly Skilled Migrant scheme in late 2025 — worth watching but the graduate threshold survives as of now; the Amsterdam financial sector is a net beneficiary of Brexit and is expanding.

#### Germany — Berlin, Munich, Frankfurt

Germany is Europe's largest labour market and Caner's only non-UK European country where he already has linguistic capital (A2/B1). The EU Blue Card shortage threshold is €45,934 (2026) for IT occupations — achievable at Berlin for experienced grads but tight for the most junior roles at smaller startups. Permanent residency is 21 months with Blue Card + B1 German, 33 months without — among the fastest in Europe.

**Berlin.** The Berlin startup scene in 2026 is post-correction but still the largest in continental Europe. Trade Republic, N26 (shrinking after compliance fines but still operating), Solana Labs Berlin, Gorillas is dead, Delivery Hero is slimmer, Zalando is slimmer — but Wayve has a Berlin presence, HuggingFace has a Berlin office, and Rust-in-production shows up at Trade Republic, Mozilla Berlin (small), and a surprising long tail of Rust-heavy infra startups. The Rust developer market in Berlin as of early 2026 was ~5 Rust-tagged listings on Berlin Startup Jobs but ~74 nationally — the cluster exists but is thinner than it sounds.

Berlin's lifestyle fit is the strongest of any German city by a wide margin. The after-hours scene is the major continental European nightlife anchor — bigger than Amsterdam, bigger than Paris, comparable to London in energy. Clubsterben is a real trend (Berghain still fine, many mid-tier venues lost during 2022-2024) but the scene remains unmatched on continental Europe for Caner's profile. Aesthetically, central Mitte and the regenerated parts of Friedrichshain / Prenzlauer Berg are mixed-scale and modern enough. Safety is "B+ with Central London caveats" — safer than NYC but with ambient property crime.

Political trajectory is the one meaningful downside. The AfD polled at 20.8% in the February 2025 federal election and is projected to hit 40% in the 2026 Saxony-Anhalt state election (ChronicleAI, 2026). At the federal level the grand coalition is holding, and immigration policy is tightening for asylum rather than skilled migration — the Blue Card scheme itself is politically untouchable because German industry depends on it. Caner should not expect his visa to be weaponised, but the longer-term 10-15 year trajectory on public mood is a live risk worth naming.

Verdict: **strong #4 candidate**. Berlin genuinely fits the profile; the A2/B1 German gives him an advantage no other candidate has; the Rust scene is thinner than hype but real; AfD trajectory is the main flag. Push German to B2 and Berlin strengthens further.

**Munich.** Apple, Google, BMW software, Cerebras Munich, Citadel Frankfurt via proximity. Junior-friendly if you hit the Blue Card threshold. Lifestyle fit is significantly weaker than Berlin — conservative Bavarian culture, weaker nightlife, Catholic public presence. Verdict: worth applying to specific target firms (Apple Munich for ML/silicon, Cerebras for AI hardware) but not a general destination.

**Frankfurt.** Citadel, Jane Street small presence, JPMC engineering, Morgan Stanley, ECB-adjacent fintech. Everything here is finance, the city is soulless outside the Bankenviertel, nightlife is very weak. Good for a 2-3 year career acceleration at a specific firm, bad as a long-term home. Verdict: apply only to specific firms, treat as a career stepping stone not a destination.

#### Switzerland — Zurich

Zurich is the single best combination of nominal compensation, tax regime, safety, and firm density in Europe — on paper. The practical problem is the quota. Switzerland maintained its 2026 non-EU allocation at 4,000 L permits and 4,500 B permits for the entire country (Fragomen 2026). Zurich canton typically gets the largest cantonal share but the 2025 utilisation for Zurich was noted as hitting cap in late autumn. This means competition for a permit is real and tips the balance away from fresh graduates toward senior hires with clear "no local alternative" justification.

The firm density for Caner's sectors is exceptional: Google Zurich (largest Google engineering office outside the US), Apple Zurich (machine learning and applied research), DeepMind Zurich, Citadel Zurich (has grown substantially since 2022), Hudson River Trading satellite, Tsinghua GenMab partnerships, OpenAI Switzerland (small but exists), Snowflake EMEA, Oracle engineering, a small but high-quality Rust startup scene (Nine Energy, Cargo-crates authors work there).

Tax: Swiss cantonal tax in Zurich canton is roughly 22-30% effective for a top-bracket grad, which is *exceptional* by European standards. Salary: Google Zurich pays Google London equivalent + ~15-20% for cost of living. Nine Elms-equivalent mixed-scale neighbourhoods exist (Europaallee by the main station is a recent regeneration). Safety is A+. Secular culture is A+. Nightlife is weak by continental standards. Fitness infrastructure is excellent.

The visa problem cannot be solved by strategy, only by leverage. For a fresh grad, the most realistic path is: target firms with strong internal relocation programmes (Google, Apple) from a London posting. A direct-to-Zurich application from London is viable but selective — the employer must prove no EU candidate exists, which for a generic role is hard but for a specialist Rust/quant role is genuinely possible.

Verdict: **stretch candidate, apply selectively**. Zurich is where Caner should want to be in 5 years via an internal Google or Apple transfer, not where he should expect to land in 2027. Trajectory: quotas flat, compensation rising, Swiss political neutrality holding, climate exposure modest, currency stability is the best in the developed world.

#### Austria — Vienna

Vienna has the Red-White-Red Card with a salary threshold around €3,225/mo (2026) which is aggressively low for a skilled migrant scheme, plus the EU Blue Card option. Shortage-occupation listing for software developers means reduced points requirement. The city is safe, secular enough, and has a strong café culture (actually one of the better café-with-laptop cultures in Europe despite Caner's concerns about continental Europe generally). But firm density for Caner's specific sectors (Rust, quant, AI infra, fintech) is thin — Vienna is a government-adjacent city with decent consulting and traditional enterprise engineering. Bitpanda is the one notable fintech. Red Bull Media House has engineering roles but they are consumer/media-facing.

Verdict: **marginal**. Visa is friendly, lifestyle is a mix of strengths and "historic-low-rise-feels-preserved-not-building" friction, firm density is the blocker. Would grade B overall.

#### France — Paris

Paris has the Talent Passport, which is a skilled-worker route with a ~€43k threshold and a 4-year permit — mechanically workable. The firm density question is mixed: Mistral AI, Hugging Face (one of the best Rust-and-ML companies anywhere), Datadog Paris, Shift Technology, Doctolib, Criteo, Societe Generale engineering. That is a serious AI infra cluster — Hugging Face alone is a near-perfect portfolio match for Caner's NeuroDrive / tinygrad work.

The aesthetic objection is real. Paris is the quintessential historic-preservation city; La Défense is a partial modern cluster but is the Canary Wharf failure mode (no mixed scale, isolated from life, weak walkability). The Parisian café culture is actively hostile to laptop work (Caner has explicitly noted this). Safety is fine central but degrades quickly at outer-ring.

Verdict: **apply only to Hugging Face and possibly Mistral**. The lifestyle fit is so poor that only a genuinely career-defining role justifies the move, and Hugging Face is that role.

#### Nordics — Stockholm, Copenhagen, Oslo, Helsinki, Reykjavik

**Stockholm.** Sweden's work permit is relatively simple — collective agreement salary levels, processing via Migrationsverket, reasonable timelines. The firm cluster is surprisingly strong: Spotify HQ (the largest tech engineering office in Scandinavia), Klarna (post-restructuring but still large), Ericsson (telecom + 5G + edge compute), King (games), Minecraft (Mojang, Microsoft), Hemnet, Izettle (acquired by PayPal but engineering in Stockholm), iZettle alumni spinoffs, Voi, and a genuinely strong AI research cluster around KTH.

Stockholm compensation is real: Spotify averages SEK ~1M total comp (~£76k) for engineers, Klarna slightly lower, and the effective tax rate is high (~55% top bracket) but flat. The 30-year tax expert ruling gives a reduced rate for a limited period to foreign specialists.

**Lifestyle fit is unexpectedly excellent.** Hammarby Sjöstad and Norra Djurgårdsstaden are textbook mixed-scale regenerated districts — exactly the KGX/Nine Elms idiom Caner explicitly grades A+. Safety is A+ (still Europe's safest major city). Secular culture is A+ (Sweden is effectively post-religious in public life). Thunderstorms are rare but the winter has snow and dramatic grey weather Caner prefers over Mediterranean summer. Nightlife is moderate — stronger than Copenhagen, weaker than Berlin. English fluency is 95%+ in daily life. The frontier-tech adoption is high (Klarna is arguably a payments frontier; Ericsson leads on 5G; AVs being tested).

Verdict: **top-10 candidate, underrated in the prior analysis**. Stockholm is a genuine dark horse. Visa is friendly, firm density is real for AI/ML/fintech, lifestyle fit is unexpectedly strong on the aesthetic axis.

**Copenhagen.** Denmark's Positive List scheme updated for 2026 includes IT occupations — viable visa path. Firms: Unity Technologies, Zendesk Copenhagen, Tradeshift, Lunar, Pleo. The cluster is smaller than Stockholm's but high-quality. Lifestyle is comparable to Stockholm but slightly more expensive. Safety A+, secular culture A+. Verdict: honourable mention, below Stockholm only on firm density.

**Oslo.** Oil-adjacent wealth, small tech scene, very high cost of living. Opera Software, Cognite (industrial data/AI), Schibsted. Too narrow for Caner's sectors. Verdict: skip.

**Helsinki.** Nokia legacy, F-Secure, Rovio, Supercell, Wolt (acquired by DoorDash). Finnish language is a hard barrier for daily integration even though English works professionally. The tech scene is thinner than Stockholm's. Verdict: skip unless Supercell graduate programme opens.

**Reykjavik.** No. Scene is too small.

#### Southern Europe — Spain, Portugal, Italy

**Barcelona / Madrid.** Spain's tech scene has been genuinely expanding — Typeform, Glovo (troubled), Cabify, King Madrid, N26 Barcelona, Amazon Web Services Madrid region. Spain introduced a startup visa and a digital nomad visa in 2023. Barcelona's tech scene is larger than Madrid's for Caner's sectors but the Spanish tax regime is unfriendly to high earners (up to 47%+). #### Central/Eastern Europe — Poland, Czech Republic

### North America

**Framing.** North America is where the firm density ceiling for Caner's sectors genuinely sits. NYC, SF, Seattle are the best tech cities on earth for this profile. But the visa mechanics for a Turkish fresh grad are brutal — US H-1B is wage-weighted now (2026) which helps high-paid Level IV applicants but does not make the lottery disappear, and Canada's Express Entry is structurally biased against fresh non-Canadian grads without ties. Every US city below is graded against the counterfactual "assuming he wins the H-1B lottery" — without that assumption, everything below is stretch-only.

#### United States

**New York City.** The highest firm density for Caner's sectors on earth. Jane Street, HRT, Citadel NY, Two Sigma, DE Shaw, Virtu, Jump Trading NYC office, Jump Crypto, Millennium, Goldman engineering, JPM engineering, Stripe NYC, Anthropic NYC, and the entirety of American fintech infrastructure. NYC is where Caner's career ceiling is genuinely the highest.

The visa problem dominates everything. The 2026 H-1B lottery introduced wage-weighted selection — Level IV applicants have 61% selection probability vs Level I at 15%. A Jane Street NYC grad salary would almost certainly register at Level III or IV given the compensation bands. The September 2025 $100,000 fee proclamation applies to consular processing but NOT to change-of-status filings — so the mechanic is "land on an F-1 OPT first, then change status from within the US," which is not Caner's situation.

The practical answer for Caner is: the US is not a direct-from-London landing. It is a 2-year-experience-to-intra-company-transfer (L-1) pathway. Jane Street, Citadel, Anthropic, Stripe all have London offices with genuine London-to-NYC transfer pipelines after 1-2 years. **If Caner wants NYC, the path is London-to-NYC, not Istanbul/London-to-NYC directly.**

Lifestyle fit is extraordinarily strong — NYC is the reference point for high-energy urbanism, safety is strong in Manhattan-below-96th, secular public culture is total, café density is the highest in the world, Waymo is not in NYC but AV testing is underway. Aesthetic: Hudson Yards is the single most KGX-like thing in North America — mixed-scale, regenerated, integrated Vessel and High Line, walkable.

Verdict: **aim for London-to-NYC transfer at 1-2 years into a first job**. Direct apply is theoretically possible but should not be the plan.

**San Francisco Bay Area.** Same visa problem. Firm density for AI infra is higher than anywhere else on earth (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google Brain, Meta AI, Nvidia HQ, Databricks, xAI, dozens of Rust-in-production infra startups — Sentry, Datadog SF, Cloudflare SF). The lifestyle mismatch is sharper than NYC: the Tenderloin and parts of SoMa actively fail the safety test, the SF housing market is still brutal, and café density / walkability outside specific enclaves is weaker than NYC. Waymo is in SF. Frontier tech adoption is the highest on earth.

Verdict: **same transfer strategy as NYC — target an Anthropic London or OpenAI London role and transfer internally**. Direct apply is a stretch.

**Seattle.** Amazon, Microsoft, Facebook Seattle, Google Seattle, Valve, Stripe Seattle, Tableau. Lifestyle match is genuinely strong — Seattle South Lake Union is exactly the mixed-scale modern regeneration Caner grades A+, safety is moderate-strong in downtown/SLU/Capitol Hill. Grey drizzle climate is a positive for him. H-1B problem applies. Verdict: would be top-3 if visa worked; direct apply is a stretch.

**Austin.** Tesla, Oracle, Indeed, Meta Austin, Apple Austin, Meta AI Austin, and a quickly-growing fintech scene. Texas has no state income tax — significant for Caner's £500k trajectory ambition. Austin's aesthetic is mid-rise boomtown with genuine walkability downtown but the car-dependency outside downtown is a real issue. Waymo launching in Austin (already running). H-1B problem applies. Verdict: interesting as a tax-advantaged post-transfer destination, not a first move.

**Miami.** Post-2020 fintech/crypto surge has largely been a bust. Citadel moved HQ to Miami but the engineering remains in NYC. The cultural fit (beach/nightlife) is one of Caner's explicit anchors. ### Asia-Pacific

**Framing.** Asia-Pacific is where the interesting moves happen 3-5 years into a career, not at graduate stage. Direct-apply pathways exist but are narrow; the dominant play is "land at a London prop shop with a Singapore/Tokyo/HK office and transfer internally."

#### Singapore

Singapore is on paper a perfect fit — the most KGX-like city in Asia (Marina Bay is exactly the mixed-scale modern regeneration with integrated green), unrivalled safety, strong secular public culture, functional English, and the densest quant/HFT cluster in Asia. Optiver Singapore, IMC Singapore, Jane Street Singapore, HRT Singapore, Jump Trading Singapore, Tower Singapore, DRW Singapore, and a growing AI infra cluster (Stripe Singapore, Anthropic Singapore opened in 2024, Temasek-funded startups).

The 2026 Employment Pass minimum salary is S$6,200/mo (~S$74,400 annual), with tech-sector minimums S$6,500-7,000. COMPASS requires 40+ points across qualifications, diversity, and local support — a fresh York BEng with a 2:2 will score moderate on qualifications (the 2:2 classification hurts here) and low on diversity for a Turkish national. The practical answer: COMPASS is designed to favour strong companies with diversity gaps. A Jane Street Singapore or Optiver Singapore sponsorship clears the bar because the company scores highly on "top-tier firm."

The lifestyle friction Caner should actually care about: nightlife is legally restricted (drink cutoffs, club closing times), the religious environment is modest but present (multi-faith public expression is more visible than Stockholm/Berlin), and the integration experience for non-locals tends to keep you in the expat enclave by default. Caner's stated integration mindset is the right attitude, but Singapore is famously a place where integration is hard regardless of your attitude.

Verdict: **direct-apply stretch, or internal transfer from London at 1-2 years**. If Jane Street Singapore or Optiver Singapore opens a fresh-grad slot, apply. Otherwise target London first, Singapore second.

#### Tokyo

Tokyo's HSP (Highly Skilled Professional) visa is surprisingly generous for young STEM grads — there are meaningful bonuses for being under 30, for having a degree from a top-300 university, and for specialised skills. Caner with a York BEng, age 24, and demonstrated project portfolio should plausibly hit 70+ points for basic HSP and potentially 80+ (which unlocks 1-year PR rather than 3-year).

The firm density for quant/HFT in Tokyo is real and under-appreciated: Optiver Tokyo, Jane Street Tokyo, HRT Tokyo, Jump Tokyo, IMC Tokyo, Tower Tokyo, Susquehanna Tokyo. These offices hire English-first and do not require Japanese for the role. The Tokyo tech-startup cluster is smaller than Singapore's but growing (Preferred Networks, Mercari, SmartNews).

Lifestyle: Tokyo is extraordinarily safe (A+ in every part of the city). Aesthetic is mixed — Shinjuku / Roppongi / Marunouchi are mixed-scale modern, older districts are smaller scale. Café-with-laptop culture is actually better than Caner's prior note suggests — Tokyo has extensive chain coffee (Starbucks, Doutor, Tully's) that allows laptop work, though traditional cafés are small. Frontier tech adoption is extreme. Waymo is testing in Tokyo. Secular public culture is strong — Shinto/Buddhist presence is cultural rather than regulatory.

The language barrier is the real friction. Daily life in Tokyo without Japanese works but keeps you in the expat/international enclave. Caner's integration mindset is the right attitude for Japan specifically — Japan rewards long-term effort at language learning, and a 24-year-old who commits to 2-3 years of serious Japanese study can build genuine integration.

Verdict: **credible direct-apply target for the trading firms, and a genuine long-term destination if the Japanese language investment gets made**.

#### Hong Kong

Hong Kong's political trajectory since 2020 is the dominant factor. The Article 23 national security law, the erosion of "one country two systems," and the ongoing departure of international firms and expatriates have materially reduced HK's attractiveness as a long-term destination. Firms: Jane Street HK still operates, Optiver HK still operates, but the tech cluster has contracted. Lifestyle fit is mixed — HK Central is the dense vertical city Caner doesn't love but recognises as "built forward," Kowloon is denser still. Tax is 15% — excellent for high earners.

Verdict: **skip for long-term. Consider only as 1-2 year internal transfer for career resume value**.

**Taipei.** The Gold Card visa is unusually graduate-friendly — if Caner can argue skills-based qualification without the NT$160k/month salary floor, the visa is genuinely fast-track. Tech cluster: TSMC (hardware-adjacent, not Caner's direct target), a growing Rust and ML startup scene, crypto and DeFi presence. Lifestyle: safe, secular, mixed-scale urban, strong café culture, taiwanese semiconductor exposure is real. Verdict: honourable mention, worth Gold Card application as speculative option.

**Shanghai / Shenzhen.** Mainland China is geopolitically fraught for a Turkish national (visa mechanics are workable but increasingly restrictive), and the firms are Alibaba/Tencent/ByteDance whose international alumni networks are strong but whose day-to-day operations are Chinese-language-dominant. Skip.

## 4. Deep Dive — Top 5 Candidates

### 4.1 London, United Kingdom

**Tier 1 factors.**

- **Visa**: Graduate visa runs until August 2027. Skilled Worker from August 2027 requires £41,700 general threshold or higher going rate (per 22 July 2025 reform), with the 2026 updates pushing some going rates higher. For a quant/fintech/AI grad role, this is easily cleared. The risk is not "can Caner clear the threshold" but "can Caner's target company sponsor," and for Jane Street, HRT, Citadel, Stripe, Anthropic London, the answer is yes and they are established sponsors. For smaller Rust-in-production startups (Evervault, TrueLayer) the sponsorship is fuzzier. **State: good with caveats. Trajectory: neutral to mildly negative as the UK tightens Skilled Worker conditions, but fintech/AI sectors are protected.**
- **Firm density**: unambiguous global top-3 for Caner's sectors outside NYC. Jane Street, HRT, Citadel, XTX, Optiver London, IMC London, Jump, Millennium, Squarepoint, G-Research, Stripe, Revolut, Thought Machine, Wise, DeepMind, Anthropic London, Isomorphic Labs, Wayve, Graphcore (Bristol), Cloudflare, TrueLayer, Evervault, Proton UK, Rerun London. Double-digit active graduate pipelines. **State: excellent. Trajectory: neutral — London's tech is recovering from 2023-2024 cuts.**

The friction is that Caner's *current* area (Croydon) is the anti-pattern, but this is a within-London move, not a between-city one. **State: excellent for central, failing for current Caner residence. Fixable.**
- **Safety**: central London is A- by profile standards. Outer London (Croydon, Tottenham, Elephant and Castle) is D by the same standards. **State: mixed, fixable with move.**
- **Political stability**: UK post-Brexit stability is good. Labour government since July 2024, immigration tightening is targeted at low-skilled routes rather than Skilled Worker tech. **State: good. Trajectory: neutral.**

**Tier 2 factors.** Nightlife is decent (better than Amsterdam/Zurich, weaker than Berlin/NYC) and on a positive trajectory via DCMS reforms. Salary × COL is tough — London is expensive but graduate quant salaries clear it. Tax is 45% top marginal. Secular culture A+. Café culture is the best in Europe. PR path via Skilled Worker is 5 years to ILR. Frontier tech: Waymo launching in London in 2026 (scheduled). Gym infrastructure: Third Space exists and is attainable on any senior-grad salary. **Tier 3 factors.** Integration is unusually good for London — a 24-year-old Turkish Londoner is not an outsider. Housing is tight but manageable with effort. Heathrow is the world's best-connected airport. Food is the world's most diverse. NHS is stressed but functional. Time zone is ideal for global work. GBP is stable.

**Trajectory outlook.** 1-3 years: fintech/AI hiring recovery continues, Waymo launches, nightlife reforms take effect. 5-7 years: firm density intact, cost of living still crushing but grad salaries keep pace, Labour's second term if re-elected continues moderate immigration policy. 10-15 years: Brexit damage mostly absorbed, climate exposure modest, London's global tech position may weaken slightly vs NYC/Singapore but will remain top-3.

**Verdict**: the optimal immediate base. **Stay through the visa clock.** The question is not "is London the best fit" (obviously yes) but "what happens in August 2027." The answer is "either convert to Skilled Worker via a sponsored grad job, or relocate via an internal transfer from that sponsored grad job to Amsterdam/Dublin/NYC/Singapore/Tokyo." The entire strategy is: **get sponsored in London, then use London as a launchpad**.

**Risks**: (a) failure to land a sponsor-licensed employer by August 2027 — mitigated by filtering all applications for sponsor licence status; (b) sudden UK immigration policy shift post-2027 — low probability but not zero; (c) Caner gets stuck in Croydon for another year and the lifestyle erosion hurts mental health.

**Why London specifically**: because no other city combines the firm density, the visa incumbency, the English language, the aesthetic reference points, and the frontier-tech trajectory (Waymo 2026, AI infra cluster, fintech rebuild). Every other city is a compromise on at least one of these axes.

### 4.2 Dublin, Ireland

Firm density is ~60% of London for his sectors (Stripe HQ, Susquehanna Dublin, Intercom, Workday, Google Dublin, Meta Dublin — though Meta cuts were significant, AWS Dublin, Accenture Labs). PR path: 5 years to long-term residence via Stamp 4. Frontier tech is weaker than London (Waymo not launching, AV testing thin). Gym infrastructure: OK, not Third Space class. English is native. Climate is grey drizzle.

**Tier 3.** Integration is generally good for English-speakers. Housing is a crisis. Dublin Airport is decent. Food scene is improving. Healthcare is mixed. Time zone matches London. EUR is stable.

**Trajectory.** 1-3 years: housing crisis continues, corporate tax base holds after 15% minimum, Stripe Dublin stable, Meta Dublin contracting. 5-7 years: net-neutral on firm density, housing may catch up. 10-15 years: Ireland's fiscal surplus gives it institutional strength; the major unknown is whether multinational tax rate harmonisation erodes Ireland's pull.

**Verdict**: the best non-UK European landing pad for a fresh BEng. Apply to Stripe Dublin and Susquehanna Dublin in parallel with London applications.

**Risks**: housing crisis erodes quality of life; Stripe layoffs reach Dublin; lifestyle scale is smaller than London.

Frontier tech: Netherlands is a strong AV adopter (WEpod testing since 2016). Gym infra: good. English: 95% functional. Climate: grey drizzle, fine.

**Risks**: height/aesthetic friction persists even in Zuidas; housing is brutal; Dutch political tightening on migration.
