## 2. Methodology

I evaluated candidates in this order: **visa-reality → career ceiling → safety → aesthetic/lifestyle**. I believe strongly that the visa question is binary and dominant for a non-EU fresh grad with a 2027 deadline. No matter how much Caner likes a city, if the visa is structurally closed it is noise, and no matter how much he dislikes a city, if the visa is wide open and the firms are there, it deserves weight.

My reasoning prioritises:

- **Visa realism for a Turkish BEng with a 2:2 and no work history.** I treat Blue Card salary thresholds, Irish CSEP graduate thresholds, Dutch HSM under-30 thresholds, Swiss quotas, and Canadian CRS maths as hard facts, not preferences.
- **Target-firm density in his actual sectors**, specifically quant/HFT, modern fintech (not payments consulting), AI infrastructure, and systems/Rust-in-production. Counting generic "tech jobs" is not useful.
- **Safety as a precondition** — I treat unsafe neighbourhoods as invalidating downstream lifestyle factors, consistent with his explicit Croydon pain point, but I do not double-count it across tiers.
- **Aesthetic friction as modulation, not veto.** Caner has strong aesthetic preferences, but the profile's "Amsterdam rejected on aesthetic" is the kind of opinion that I want to stress-test against the career-density argument. A 24-year-old can live with a low-rise aesthetic for two years at Optiver if the career payoff is right.
- **Trajectory over snapshot**, per the rubric. London's 2027 visa reform and UK nightlife uptrend, Berlin's Clubsterben downtrend, the US H-1B weighted reform, the EU Blue Card reform, and Canada's Express Entry STEM-category change are all load-bearing for the recommendation.

What I deliberately de-weighted: climate (he explicitly says he doesn't care beyond extremes), Turkish-food accessibility (minor and widely available in Western cities), and airport connectivity to Istanbul (he explicitly says this is not a factor).

My own angle: **London is not just the default, it is the answer.** Every other city in my top 10 is a plan-B or a 2028+ transfer. The whole structure of this analysis assumes his first job is in London, not that he is going to cold-apply to Sydney and get an offer. The geographic diversification is about what happens when the Graduate visa clock runs out, not about choosing a first move.

## 3. Continent-by-continent sweep

### Europe

Europe is the centre of gravity. Language-accessible, culturally closer to Istanbul, visa paths for non-EU STEM talent are the most standardised on earth, and the concentration of quant/HFT/modern-fintech that actually matches Caner's portfolio is the highest globally outside the US. Within Europe I weight the UK and Ireland above the continent because of English, but the EU options are real.

Quant/HFT — Jane Street, HRT, Citadel, Citadel Securities, Optiver London, IMC London, XTX, Jump, DRW, GSA, Man Group, Squarepoint, QRT, Point72 Cubist, Balyasny. Modern fintech — Stripe, Wise, Revolut, Monzo, Thought Machine, GoCardless, Checkout, TrueLayer. #### Ireland

**Dublin.** This is the most mechanically achievable non-UK move, full stop. The CSEP graduate salary threshold is **€36,848 from 1 March 2026** — set deliberately low for recent graduates in critical skills occupations, which includes software developer. Stripe's Dublin HQ is hiring new grads. SIG Dublin is running a 2026 Software Developer Graduate programme openly targeting the exact profile. Fenergo, Susquehanna, HubSpot, and the big tech EMEA HQs (Google, Meta, LinkedIn, Amazon) are all Dublin-based. Stamp 4 (effectively PR) at 2 years is the fastest pathway in Europe.

Stripe is the single biggest anchor; SIG is a real quant fund; after that the density drops fast. No Jane Street, no HRT, no Citadel, no Google DeepMind-class AI. (3) Aesthetic is fine but not futuristic — no Kings Cross analogue, although Docklands/Silicon Docks is decent.

Verdict: **tier 2.** The single safest non-UK move. If London does not convert, Dublin is the answer.

#### Netherlands — Amsterdam

I am going to actively override the profile rejection here because the career ceiling is too high to leave on aesthetic grounds alone.

**Visa:** Dutch HSM under-30 threshold is **€4,357/month excluding holiday allowance = €56,640+ gross/year** in 2026. This is trivially achievable at Optiver (new-grad comp well above that), IMC, Flow Traders, Adyen, Databricks, Booking, and even smaller Dutch fintech. The visa is employer-sponsored, decided in weeks, and comes with the 30% ruling tax break for up to 5 years, which makes effective tax on Caner's first salary lower than London.

**Career density:** Optiver HQ is Amsterdam. IMC HQ is Amsterdam. Flow Traders HQ is Amsterdam. DaVinci Derivatives, Maven Securities, Da Vinci are all Amsterdam. Adyen, the second-biggest payments company in Europe, is Amsterdam. Booking.com, which operates on Google-scale infrastructure, is Amsterdam. Databricks EMEA, Uber EMEA, Netflix EMEA are Amsterdam. For Nyquestro-specifically (matching engine, lock-free, exchange protocols), **Amsterdam has higher target-firm density than London in the HFT subspecialism**, because the HQs live there.

**Safety:** per the 2026 research, Amsterdam's safety index is 71, which is comfortably above London's baseline. The city centre is safe to walk at midnight, petty crime exists but violent crime is low. It passes the safety gate cleanly.

**Lifestyle friction:**
- Height/aesthetic: the Dutch male average is ~185cm and the city is low-rise historic. This is the crux of the prior rejection. I acknowledge it's a real friction, but I don't think it's a career-veto when weighed against Optiver's fit. Most of Amsterdam's tech offices are in Zuidas, which is a more modern high-rise business district — not Kings Cross, but not Jordaan either. Zuidas is the mitigating factor the prior analysis underweighted.
- Nightlife: decent and active, particularly in the international crowd. Not Berlin-class, but not restrained either.
- Café culture: strong. Amsterdam has a real laptop-friendly café scene.

**Trajectory:** the 30% ruling is being reduced (to 27% from 2027), the housing market is very tight, and PVV politics introduce anti-immigration uncertainty. But for a 2-year bet at Optiver this doesn't materially affect Caner.

**My independent verdict:** Amsterdam is a strong candidate, not a reject. The profile's aesthetic rejection is legitimate but I believe it was overweighted. **If Optiver Amsterdam or IMC Amsterdam offers him a graduate SWE role, he should take it**, and deal with the Jordaan-scale friction by living in a modern Zuidas flat and treating Amsterdam as a 2-year career accelerator before transferring to Optiver Singapore or Optiver Chicago. Tier 2 in my ranking.

### North America

**The dominant constraint is the H-1B lottery.** Per the 2026 research, DHS has implemented a wage-weighted selection process starting Feb 2026: Level I (entry-level) positions get 1 lottery entry, Level II gets 2, Level III gets 3, Level IV gets 4. Entry-level wages in SF/NYC at $110-150k are Level I or II. A fresh CS grad at a standard starting salary has roughly 15-25% selection odds — down from the old flat 25-30%. The system now actively discriminates against entry-level hires in high-cost cities. **This is catastrophic for Caner's H-1B odds at a first job in NYC/SF/Seattle.** The system is now optimised against him.

The alternative routes:
- **L-1 intracompany transfer** after 1+ year at a London office of a US firm (Stripe London → Stripe SF, Citadel London → Citadel NY, etc.) This bypasses the lottery entirely. **This is the realistic route.**
- **O-1 extraordinary ability** — technically possible but very demanding evidence-wise for a 24-year-old with no work history.
- **TN visa** — Canada/Mexico only, not Turkey.

So: any North American analysis for a *first* job is a stretch. For a *second* job after 1-2 years at a transferable London office, it becomes real.

**New York City.** Career density: Jane Street, HRT, Citadel, Two Sigma, DE Shaw, Jump, Tower, Virtu, Goldman Sachs tech, JP Morgan tech, Bridgewater, Millennium, Point72, Radix, Bloomberg, Databricks, Anthropic NY, Stripe NY. **The highest-density quant/fintech city on earth.** Safety varies by neighbourhood: Manhattan south of 96th and Brooklyn north of Prospect Park are safe; some subway stations and parts of the Bronx/Queens periphery are not. Lifestyle is unmatched for nightlife and aesthetic match (the NYC "mixed-scale + mass + integrated greenery" pattern of Hudson Yards, Battersea Park City, Hell's Kitchen, West Chelsea matches Kings Cross precisely). Tax is high (NY state + city ~50%). Frontier tech as a user: excellent, Waymo is in NYC now, modern payments everywhere. **Verdict: tier 1 lifestyle and career, tier 3 access — target as an L-1 transfer from a London office, not a day-one move.**

**San Francisco / Bay Area.** Career density: Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Meta, Apple, Stripe HQ, Databricks, Scale AI, Modal, Lambda, Cerebras, SambaNova, Groq, Rerun (Rust-native), every AI infra company in existence. Safety: central SF has severe visible disorder (Tenderloin, parts of SoMa, Mid-Market), which fails Caner's safety bar; suburban Bay Area is fine. Aesthetic: Downtown SF fails almost every test he has, and the suburban sprawl fails the walkability test. Lifestyle friction is real. **Verdict: the career density is the best in the world, but SF as a place to live is a poor fit for this profile. Stretch target via Stripe London → Stripe SF transfer specifically, not a direct apply.**

Safety: central Seattle has visible disorder issues similar to SF but less severe; Bellevue is fine. ### Asia-Pacific

#### Singapore

Per the 2026 research, the Employment Pass minimum is **S$5,600/month for non-financial, S$6,200 for financial** sectors, and the COMPASS scoring system gives software engineers 15-20 points which is favourable. Caner's age works in his favour (EP salary expectations scale up with age). The realistic problem is that Singapore firms don't typically open EP for fresh non-local grads — they prefer to hire 2-3 years of experience — but the quant/HFT firms (Optiver Singapore, IMC Singapore, Jane Street Singapore, Jump Singapore, HRT Singapore) do take both graduate hires and London-transfer hires. **The London-to-Singapore transfer is the cleanest realistic path**, which is why it shows up in my top 10 as a 2028+ target rather than a 2026 target.

Frontier tech adoption is excellent. Climate is equatorial (continuous heat with very heavy AC — matches the "Dubai-style luxury with AC is fine" framing).

**Verdict: tier 2 as a 2028-2029 transfer target from London.**
